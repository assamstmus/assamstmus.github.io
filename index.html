Manjil Hazarika  

Speaker


He did his PhD on ethno-linguistic pre-historic archaeology from the University of Berne, Switzerland. He is part of the Indo-French team studying human origins in the Indian subcontinent, which discovered animal fossils bearing man-made cut marks dating back 2.6 million years in the Shivalik Hills. This pushes back the antiquity of human presence in India by about a million years.



The gaze of the Colonial Anthropologists have influenced the representation and the very construct of Northeast India. The anthropologist's endeavour can be understood as a specifically Western enterprise based on an idea of rational scientific discourse. In an imperialist, colonial context, the project of anthropology is to create an "other" and to turn difference into distance. To further this the nationalist policy of isolating, reconstituting and the creation of the North East India on the basis of viewing its people as primitive gave rise to an identity politics based on othering. However before and beyond national borders it was (and is) a very transnational region. The river Brahmaputra and its valley are a fluid space - the river connecting major parts of the area and being the carrier of people, goods, imagination and lore, and the valley a frontier and a big landmass between South and Southeast Asia.



The region of North East India can be viewed as the South of the Global South. The rhetoric of a region that is “underdeveloped”, “isolated”, “frontier”, “ethnically different” and “physically distant” stick on. Thus it is distant in the popular and “mainstream” imagination too. In its larger scope the project Museums are closed at nights seeks to examine the conflict between the Global North and Global South, where the confrontation between rival knowledge systems today needs to be reexamined.




The Assam State Museum was established on 21st April, 1940, by the Kamarupa Anusandhana Samiti or the Assam Research Society, Guwahati. It was the only institute at that time that undertook researches in the history and culture of this part of the country, particularly Assam. Founded in 1912, it was the result of tireless endeavours of a number of persons genuinely interested in history and culture. The project aims at ‘decolonizing’ the cultural memory of the museum and open up the space for the popular, pleasant and indigenous knowledge. Benjamin writes, “In the act of collecting it is decisive that the object be dissociated from all of its original functions in order to enter into the closest possible relationship with its equivalents.” The basic question that project asks is – What role can art play in reimagining, and reorganizing the imperialist colonialist conception of culture and cultural memory?

Museums are profoundly political both in what is included and excluded. The project looks as the memory function of a museum as an endeavour to induce amnesia by exclusions and by taking away a lived experience of the objects and artifacts. Through the revoking, digging out and bringing back into the public discourse certain objects in the museum we propose to give them a "second life" and activating them by inviting artists, historians and public intellectuals to engage with them in different ways.
Some of the methods for this will be to open up the collection for multiple readings and exploration of different points of view. For this we will invite artists, designer, filmmakers, academicians, architect and scientists thereby creating a space for hybrid practices.
